Pranav Misra, Ph.D.

Cognitive Neuroscience · AI · Language

Ph.D. researcher studying multimodal representations of language in the human brain using invasive recordings and modern AI models.

About

I am from Lucknow, India, and Cambridge, Massachusetts — two places that have shaped my worldview through wholesome chai talks and intellectual discourse. My work is guided by three simple principles: kindness, truthfulness, and unbridled curiosity.

My path into the science of intelligence began at BITS Pilani, where I built a foundation in logic, computation, and algorithms, then wandered into gravitational theory and advanced geometry simply because the questions felt compelling. I later returned to computation through neural networks and fuzzy logic, working on augmented reality and visual segmentation for safer surgeries and earning the General Electric Foundation Fellowship.

At Harvard, I combined deep learning with human psychophysics to study visual episodic memory and then moved to invasive recordings in human patients. My Ph.D. work investigates how parts of speech, syntax, and meaning are represented in the brain, integrating tools from language theory, machine learning, and systems neuroscience.

Beyond research, I play soccer, practice Indian classical flute, and dance tango — reminders that intelligence is not just about computation, but also about embodiment, rhythm, and shared experience.

Natural & Artificial Intelligence

Broadly, I am interested in how intelligent computation emerges in biological and artificial systems. On the biological side, I study intracranial signals during language tasks to understand how the brain composes structure from words, phrases, and multimodal stimuli. On the artificial side, I work with recurrent networks and modern language models to probe how formal grammars, compositionality, and hierarchy emerge from training.

I am particularly drawn to questions where symbolic descriptions — like grammars or task hierarchies — meet dynamical systems and neural networks. How do discrete linguistic or cognitive programs manifest in continuous neural dynamics? What architectures and learning rules make this mapping robust, data-efficient, and biologically plausible?

My long-term goal is to develop theory-informed models that connect neurocognitive frameworks (“what is useful to model?”) with mechanistic accounts of computation (“how is it actually implemented?”), in service of a deeper science of intelligence.

Ph.D. in Biophysics — Harvard University (2019–2025)

Thesis: Robust and Multimodal Intracranial Signals for Language in the Human Brain

  • Large-scale sEEG recordings from 5,064 electrodes in 47 patients across 100+ hours of linguistic stimuli.
  • Neural evidence for theories of parts-of-speech, syntax, and semantic composition using multimodal tasks.
  • Applied language and vision–language models to improve classification accuracy and interpretability of neural signals.
  • Developed cross-modal decoding (sentence ↔ image) and data-efficient learning across subjects.

Clinical Collaboration Lead — Cleveland Clinic & Boston Children’s Hospital

  • Identified invasive neural signatures of multimodal sentence processing relevant to epilepsy surgery planning.
  • Compressed a multi-institutional clinical study timeline from ~3 years to ~6 months by removing logistical bottlenecks.

Teaching — Neuro 140: Biological & Artificial Intelligence

  • Mentored 60+ undergraduate and graduate students from biomedical engineering, CS, and data science.
  • Guided projects involving autoencoders, semi-supervised learning, and cross-modal models for biological data.
