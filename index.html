About

I am a postdoctoral fellow working on the geometry and structure of language representations in large neural networks. I study how concepts, categories and compositional structure are organized in high-dimensional spaces, and I explore theoretical principles that explain why some representations remain stable, identifiable and generalizable.

My PhD in Biophysics at Harvard focused on language in the human brain. I built and analyzed what is to date the largest multimodal intracranial dataset for language, covering phrases, sentences, images and naturalistic meaning. That work revealed a cortical region with reliable sensitivity to parts of speech and basic syntactic structure across individuals. My academic path began at BITS Pilani, where I studied physics, mathematics and computer science. Early projects ranged from geometry and gravitational theory to neural networks and augmented reality for safer surgical planning. These experiences shaped the broad question that drives my research today: how structured computation in language can be formally defined, represented and understood across both biological and artificial systems.
