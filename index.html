About

I am a postdoctoral fellow at Harvard University, working on the geometry and structure of language representations in large neural networks. I study how concepts, categories and compositional structure organize in high-dimensional spaces, and explore the theoretical principles that explain why some representations are stable, identifiable, and generalizable.

My Ph.D. training in Biophysics at Harvard centered on human intracranial recordings. I built and analyzed the largest multimodal intracranial dataset for language to date, spanning minimal phrases, full sentences, images, and naturalistic meaning. That project revealed a cortical region with reliable sensitivity to parts of speech and basic syntactic structure across individuals. Prior to graduate school, I studied physics, mathematics and computer science at BITS Pilani. During that time, I worked on everything from geometry and gravitational theory to neural networks and augmented reality for safer surgical planning. Those experiences shaped the question that guides my work today: how can structured computation in language be formulated, represented and understood with principled rigor in both biological and artificial systems.
